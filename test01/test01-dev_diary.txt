DEV DIARY — Floor Plan Wall Detection Pipeline
First Approach Log
================================================
 
--- Test 01 ---
 
Started with a scanned architectural floor plan. Single-story house, roughly rectangular,
with dimension annotations on the left margin: 4.5m, 2m, 3.2m, 0.5m, 0.3m.
The plan includes furniture symbols, doors, and interior walls. The goal is clear in theory:
extract wall geometry and feed it into a Blender scene procedurally.
 
First instinct: treat it as a pixel classification problem. Walls are black, background is
white, everything else is noise. Should be straightforward.
 
It was not straightforward.
 
--- Attempt 1 — Raw threshold ---
 
Applied a strict binary threshold to isolate black pixels. Got walls, yes, but also got
furniture outlines, hatch patterns, door arcs, stair symbols, dimension lines. Basically
everything in the drawing that wasn't white came through. The output was unusable as
geometry — too much noise mixed into what should be clean wall segments.
 
Note to self: need to distinguish between "thick structural walls" and "thin annotation lines."
Wall thickness in the image is visually significant. Should exploit that.
 
--- Attempt 2 — Thickness-based filtering ---
 
Tried filtering by stroke width. The logic: real walls have a consistent thickness,
annotation lines and furniture are thinner. Used morphological operations to probe stroke
widths. Improvement, but still messy. Interior partition walls and outer walls bleed into
each other. Junction points (T-intersections, corners) create weird blobs.
 
Also noticed at this stage: the image has no consistent pixel-per-meter calibration.
The dimension annotations are there (4.5m spans a certain pixel range on the left side),
but I hadn't used them yet. That's a problem I was deferring — mistake.
 
--- Attempt 3 — Grid overlay for calibration ---
 
Decided to tackle the scale problem first before trying to be clever about detection.
Overlaid a red pixel grid at 25px intervals to manually read off the coordinate system.
Cross-referenced with the known dimension markers in the image.
 
This produced `plan_with_grid.png` and `plan_grid_precise.png`. Tedious but necessary.
Now at least I know that the building footprint spans from roughly x=150 to x=975 pixels
and y=60 to y=590 pixels at the image resolution I'm working with. That gives me an anchor.
 
--- Attempt 4 — Outer perimeter first strategy ---
 
Changed approach. Instead of trying to detect all walls at once, focus only on the outer
perimeter first. The outer walls are the longest, most continuous black regions and form
a closed polygon. If I can get those right, interior walls become a secondary problem.
 
Traced the outer boundary manually-assisted: found the four main corner coordinates,
then let the algorithm walk the perimeter by following connected black pixels.
 
First result: `outer_walls_strict.png`. Green line follows the outer wall but with gaps
at window openings and door thresholds. The perimeter is broken wherever there's an opening
in the wall. The line would jump, skip, or drift inward.
 
Not good enough for Blender — I need closed loops, not open chains.
 
--- Attempt 5 — Snapping and gap-closing ---
 
Introduced a snapping step. The idea: after detecting approximate wall centerlines,
snap all endpoints to a discrete grid aligned to the known real-world dimensions.
This forces walls to align to a clean coordinate system and closes small gaps.
 
Result: `outer_walls_snapped.png`. Better. The line is more continuous. But new problem —
the snap grid introduces its own errors. Walls that are slightly diagonal in the original
drawing get forced onto horizontal/vertical axes, sometimes visibly wrong at corners.
 
The top-left L-shaped notch in the floorplan is a recurring pain point. The algorithm
keeps misreading the interior step as part of the outer wall or skipping it entirely.
 
--- Attempt 6 — Correction pass ---
 
Added a manual correction layer on top of the snapped result. Identified the problem
segments (mostly the re-entrant corner on the left side and the notch at the center-top
where an entry/corridor breaks the top wall) and hardcoded adjusted coordinates for those
segments only.
 
`outer_walls_corrected.png` — perimeter is now closed and mostly correct.
The green line traces: top wall (with the central break), right wall, bottom wall, left wall,
and the interior notch on the top-left. It's not elegant but it works.
 
--- Attempt 7 — Full wall set ---
 
With the outer perimeter working, moved back to the interior walls.
Tried two strategies in parallel:
 
Strategy A (`all_segs_strict.png`): strict threshold + length filter. Only keep
detected line segments longer than N pixels. Catches most walls but misses short
connecting segments and junction connectors.
 
Strategy B (`all_walls_green.png`): looser threshold, then cluster and merge nearby
parallel segments into single representative lines. More complete coverage, but prone
to doubling — outer wall gets detected twice (inner face and outer face both come through).
 
Neither is clean on its own. The interior walls around the bathroom cluster (center of plan)
are particularly problematic — lots of short segments, T-junctions, and the toilet/basin
fixtures adding visual noise right next to structural walls.
 
--- End of Session Notes ---
 
What I have so far:
- A calibrated pixel coordinate system with known real-world scale
- A closed outer perimeter polygon (with manual corrections)
- Partial interior wall detection (noisy, needs merging logic)
 
What I still need:
- Clean centerline extraction for ALL walls, deduped
- Junction point topology (which walls connect to which)
- Door/window opening positions extracted as gaps in walls
- Export format compatible with the Blender pipeline
 
The core issue throughout has been that the drawing was made for human reading, not
machine parsing. Every ambiguity a human resolves visually in a millisecond (is this
thick line a wall or a heavy furniture outline?) is a genuine algorithmic problem.
 
Next session: look into Hough Line Transform as an alternative to pixel-walking.
Might get cleaner straight-line extraction directly without the perimeter-tracing approach.
Also need to start thinking about the Blender side — what data structure does the
geometry importer actually expect?
